Nancy Folbre:Pioneering Feminist Economist and Advocate for Care Work Recognition

2023-12-10 PV:

PERSONAL EXPERIENCE

Nancy Folbre is a distinguished feminist economist renowned for her exploration of how non-market production contributes to human and community development as well as economic growth. Her research encompasses various aspects, including family, gender, political status, and other power relations, expanding the definitions of human and social capital. A former economics professor at the University of Massachusetts Amherst, she is also a recipient of the MacArthur Foundation’s “genius grant.” Serving as the president of the International Association for Feminist Economics (IAFFE) from 2002 to 2003, she has been an associate editor of the journal Feminist Economics since 1995. Additionally, she is a member of the editorial board of the Journal of Women, Politics & Policy and delivered the inaugural Ailsa McKay Lecture in 2016.

Born on July 19, 1952, in San Antonio, Texas, Folbre grew up in a middle-class family. Her father, an oil lawyer, managed personal and business affairs for the Texas oil dynasty, while her mother, an advocate for freedom and knowledge, passed away when Folbre was 18. The family employed a nanny from Mexico named “Tía,” who developed a profound bond with Folbre. Upon Tía’s passing, she bequeathed her life savings of around $8,000 to the young girl. Folbre’s father invested the money in Texaco stock, covering the entire cost of her college and graduate education in public universities in Texas and Massachusetts.

Displaying a tendency to resist authority and social conventions from a young age, Folbre engaged in various political movements and social activities, developing an interest in issues of gender and class inequality. Initially studying philosophy at the University of Texas at Austin, she was arrested for participating in protests but later released on bail by a philosophy professor. After graduating, she found philosophy not practical enough for her goals. Wanting to contribute to the political changes of the era while supporting herself, she decided to study economics. Initially focusing on inequality and economic demography in a master’s program in Latin American Studies, she went on to earn a Ph.D. in economics from the University of Massachusetts Amherst.

In an interview (“The Economics of Socialist Feminism: An Interview with Nancy Folbre” by Nancy Folbre, C.J. Polychroniou), Folbre mentioned the economists who influenced her the most, including Paul Sweezy and her doctoral supervisors Sam Bowles and Jane Humphries. These individuals played an irreplaceable role in shaping her early theoretical framework. As she delved into feminist theories, Heidi Hartmann, Barbara Bergmann, Diane Elson, family bargaining ideas (mainly from Robert Pollak and Shelly Lundberg), and human capabilities (Amartya Sen) also significantly impacted her thinking. Her involvement in the activities of the International Association for Feminist Economics and the Union for Radical Political Economics further influenced her perspective.

Over the decades, Folbre, who served as a postdoctoral fellow with the Yale Economic Growth Center (EGC) from 1979 to 1980, conducted groundbreaking research on the value of “care work.” This includes childcare, eldercare, and other caregiving aspects often performed by women, frequently unpaid or low-paid. Folbre’s research combines economic analysis with feminist theory, with a specific focus on gender and family dynamics within households. One of her central arguments throughout her career is that traditional economic models inadequately consider the social and economic value of care workers like Tía. Acknowledging unpaid work as a crucial part of the economy and advocating for its more equitable redistribution necessitates renegotiating gendered divisions of labor in personal and family life. It also requires demanding public accountability for caring responsibilities and greater workplace support. Folbre emphasizes the need for increased investment in healthcare, education, childcare, aged care, and a basic minimum income. She encourages creative thinking about facility design to give both caregivers and care recipients a voice and choice. Folbre contends that traditional economic models do not adequately incorporate the social and economic value of caregivers. Her research blends economic analysis and feminist theory, emphasizing gender and family dynamics within families. She also explores the relationship between patriarchy and capitalism, highlighting the oppression of women by nation-states. Folbre argues that while the development of capitalism has weakened patriarchy and granted women more personal freedoms, it has also left mothers and other caregivers economically vulnerable.

During her time at the University of Massachusetts, Folbre was drawn to the emerging field of feminist economics, challenging traditional economic methods and assumptions from a gendered perspective. Her dissertation analyzed declining fertility rates in 18th-century New England, a period of significant change marked by the departure of young men seeking fortunes in the West, altering gender ratios and creating labor shortages on family farms. Folbre traced how these shifts expanded the role and importance of women’s work beyond child-rearing, leading to smaller families, delayed marriages, and greater economic independence for women—a insight still relevant today for many low- and middle-income countries.

After completing her doctoral degree, Folbre became a postdoctoral researcher at EGC in New Haven. She collaborated closely with Gustav Ranis and engaged in debates on Marxist economics with John Roemer at Yale University. Working alongside two female economists, Kathryn Anderson, currently at Vanderbilt University, and the late M. Anne Hill from the City University of New York, resulted in fond memories.

During her scholarship period, Folbre authored three foundational papers. Firstly, she challenged the notion of treating the family as a singular decision-making unit, emphasizing negotiations and “family bargaining” within households. Secondly, she critiqued Marxist theory, using detailed analysis to illustrate the exploitation of women by men in households, similar to the exploitation of workers by corporations. Thirdly, she underscored the significance of feminism in economics, providing empirical evidence related to the political economy of reproductive decision-making. These papers exemplify Folbre’s interdisciplinary approach, addressing mainstream economists, left-wing economists, and feminist scholars not yet interested in economics, laying the groundwork for her career.

Folbre is a founding member of the International Association for Feminist Economics and a co-founder editor of the journal Feminist Economics. She has published several articles in media such as The New York Times and authored several books, including The Invisible Heart, Who’s Minding the Kids?, Greed, Lust and Gender: A History of Economic Ideas, For Love and Money, and The Rise and Decline of Patriarchal Capitalism. Her research has had a profound impact on academic and social movements, earning her recognition as an early feminist economist.

FEMINIST ECONOMICS AND ITS EVOLUTION

Feminist economists aim to fundamentally transform the field of economics by reevaluating its assumptions, subject matter, methodology, and objectives through the integration of a gender-aware perspective. They critique the core tenets of neoclassical economics, which often consider individuals as selfish and rational actors, focus on exchange in an analytical framework, and address the fundamental problem as the allocation of resources. Instead, feminist economists assert alternative viewpoints:

Unit of Analysis: Feminist economists shift the unit of analysis from individuals to institutions and social classes.

View of Humans: Human beings are seen as socially embedded, influenced by multiple mechanisms beyond mere self-interest.

Analytical Framework: The analytical framework expands to include reproduction, production, and distribution, moving beyond the narrow focus on exchange.

Fundamental Problem: The fundamental problem is identified as the reproduction of the species, emphasizing sustainable and inclusive development.

Feminist economists raise concerns about the lack of representation of women in economic questions and models, the absence of women within the discipline of economics, and masculine biases in economic models, methods, topics, and pedagogy. They advocate for feminist principles in the discipline, such as valuing equality over efficiency, recognizing the importance of caring work versus paid work, promoting inclusiveness over exclusivity, and analyzing social norms.

Two key insights utilized by feminist economists are interdisciplinarity and intersectionality. Interdisciplinarity involves incorporating insights from various disciplines, while intersectionality provides a contextual framework for examining interconnected factors that influence power, privilege, oppression, experiences, and opportunities. These approaches help challenge conventional social norms.

A prominent aspect of feminist economists’ work involves analyzing economic processes, not just outcomes. They investigate why gender gaps exist by emphasizing the differences among people’s groups, contrasting evidence with standard economic theories.

Nancy Folbre’s work aligns with feminist economists’ objectives. She addresses two main topics: defining and measuring care work and understanding patriarchal systems. In contrast to neoclassical economists who often overlook care work, Folbre emphasizes its existence and value. Additionally, she contributes to conceptualizing patriarchal systems, recognizing their significant relationship with care work. This aligns with the broader feminist goal of achieving gender equality in economics by addressing these overlooked aspects.
