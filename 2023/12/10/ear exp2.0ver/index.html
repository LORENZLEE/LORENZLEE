2023-12-10 PV:

Nancy Folbre is a renowned feminist economist who explores how non-market production contributes to human and community development and economic growth. Her research covers aspects such as family, gender, political status, and other power relations, and expands the definition of human and social capital. She was a professor of economics at the University of Massachusetts Amherst and a recipient of the MacArthur Foundation’s “genius grant”.

Folbre was born on July 19, 1952, and grew up in a middle-class family in San Antonio, Texas. Her father was an oil lawyer who handled personal and business affairs for the Texas oil dynasty. Her mother was a woman who encouraged her to pursue freedom and knowledge, but died of illness when she was 18. Her family hired a nanny from Mexico, called “Tía”, who had a deep bond with Folbre. When she died, she left her entire savings - about $8,000 - to Folbre, which later paid for her college and graduate education.

Folbre had a tendency to resist authority and social conventions since childhood, and participated in various political movements and social activities, becoming interested in issues of gender and class inequality. She studied philosophy at the University of Texas at Austin and was arrested for participating in protests before being released on bail by a philosophy professor. After graduation, she felt that philosophy was not practical enough. She wanted to participate in the broad political changes of that era, but she also needed to support herself. She decided to study economics, first focusing on inequality and economic demography in a master’s program in Latin American Studies, then earning a PhD in economics from the University of Massachusetts Amherst. She mentioned in the interview (The Economics of Socialist Feminism: An Interview with Nancy Folbre by: Nancy Folbre, C.J. Polychroniou) that the economists who had the greatest influence on her were first of all Paul Sweezy and her doctoral supervisors Sam Bowles and Jane Humphries , these three people played an irreplaceable role in her early theoretical framework. As she gradually came into contact with more feminist theories, Heidi Hartmann, Barbara Bergmann, and Diane Elson also had an important impact on her thinking. In addition, she was also influenced by ideas such as family bargaining (mainly Robert Pollak and Shelly Lundberg) and human capabilities (Amartya Sen). Later, it was also affected by the activities of the International Association for Feminist Economics and the Union for Radical Political Economics.

Folbre’s research focuses on women’s unpaid care work. Unpaid work is an important part of the economy, and if we are to fully recognize this and redistribute it more equitably, it will require some renegotiation of the gendered division of labor in personal and family life, and a demand for public accountability for caring for others. Greater support at work. This is coupled with the need for increased investment in the provision of health care, education, childcare and aged care, as well as a basic minimum income. We also need to think more creatively about facility design, giving both caregivers and care recipients a voice and choice. She believes traditional economic models do not adequately take into account the social and economic value of caregivers. Her research combines economic analysis and feminist theory and emphasizes the role of gender and family dynamics in families. She also explores the relationship between patriarchy and capitalism and the oppression of women by nation-states, arguing that the development of capitalism has weakened patriarchy and given women more personal freedoms, but has also left mothers and other caregivers in the economic more vulnerable.

Folbre is a founding member of the International Association for Feminist Economics, and a co-founder editor of the journal Feminist Economics. She has published several articles in media such as The New York Times, and also published several books, including The Invisible Heart, Who’s Minding the Kids?, Greed, Lust and Gender: A History of Economic Ideas, For Love and Money, and The Rise and Decline of Patriarchal Capitalism. Her research has had a profound impact on the academic and social movements, and she is hailed as “an early feminist economist”.
